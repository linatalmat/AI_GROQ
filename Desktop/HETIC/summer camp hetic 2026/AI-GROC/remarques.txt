Finding 1:
Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs (1937) is the most successful Disney film ever made in inflation-adjusted dollars — $5.23 billion. No Disney film in the 85 years since has come close. The top 6 films on the all-time list are all from 1937–1961. The Golden Age wasn't just a creative peak — it was a commercial one that has never been replicated.

Finding 2:
Disney went from releasing 1 film in the 1930s to 236 films in the 1990s — a 236× increase in output. But average inflation-adjusted gross collapsed from  5.2billionperfilm∗∗(1930s)to∗∗ 75 million per film (1990s). They traded quality for volume, and the data makes that visible in a single bar chart.

Finding 3:
The Disney Renaissance of the early 1990s — Beauty and the Beast (1991), Aladdin (1992), The Lion King (1994) — was the first time since the 1960s that Disney produced films capable of entering the all-time top 15. The Lion King at $761M is the highest-grossing film from any decade after 1967, and the only 1990s film to crack the top 10. One film carried an entire era.

Finding 4:
The dark era of the 1970s–early 1980s is visible not in the low averages (hidden by volume) but in the names: The Black Hole (1979), The Fox and the Hound (1981), The Black Cauldron (1985). These were films a generation forgot. The studio that invented the animated feature film nearly lost the ability to make one. It took new leadership, new technology, and one story about a lion to bring it back.
